# Unit 1 Quest Design Guide

## Unit hub: Atlantic Crossroads

The Unit 1 hub is intentionally **not** a generic town square. It is a historical expedition map because 1491–1607 centers on contact across real places: regional Native societies, the Caribbean, Mesoamerica, the Atlantic, Europe, West Africa, and the English coast.

The player’s customizable settlement remains part of the persistent left-side HUD. The quest map is a field space that changes with each APUSH unit.

## Difficulty pattern

Students may begin with any open landmark. The instructional sequence should normally be:

1. Southwest Survey
2. Maize Ledger
3. Columbus’s Dispatch
4. New Atlantic Chart
5. Tenochtitlan Under Watch
6. Conquest & Conscience
7. Virginia: Promise or Projection?
8. Empire’s Reckoning

A production version can add prerequisite rules, but keeping early tasks open supports student agency and catch-up work.

## Writing boss progression

The Boss Battle contains all three writing modes so students repeatedly see the actual AP point totals.

- **SAQ Skirmish:** structured three-part response; useful early in the unit.
- **LEQ Duel:** standalone argument; use after students have learned contextualization and line of reasoning.
- **DBQ Siege:** seven documents plus explicit sourcing and outside-evidence checkpoints.

## Assessment recommendation

Use the quests in three distinct ways:

- **Practice:** hints, retries, XP, source annotations, and model responses available.
- **Checkpoint:** no hints until submission; students see feedback after completing the set.
- **Graded writing:** teacher scores the saved essay against the rubric; game rewards are granted only after the student demonstrates the target skill.

Never use class leaderboard placement as an academic grade.
